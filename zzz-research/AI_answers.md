Historical financials

Revenue stability

Customer concentration

Recurring vs one-time revenue

Profitability trends

B. Credit History

Business credit reports (e.g., D&B, Experian)

Why the loan is needed

Expected ROI or impact

Appropriateness of loan type

Short-term loan for working capital

Term loan for equipment

Insurance coverage

# HOW BANKS DETERMINE LOAN AMOUNT TO GIVE OUT

In Mexico, banks don’t use a single fixed formula to determine how much to lend to a PYME (SME). Instead, they combine cash-flow capacity, risk assessment, and collateral into a credit decision. Revenue matters, but it’s not the primary driver by itself.

Below is how it usually works in practice.

1. The primary metric: Debt Service Capacity

Mexican banks focus on whether the business can comfortably pay the loan, not just how big it is.

Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR)

This is the most important metric.

	DSCR
	=
	Net Operating Cash Flow
	Annual Debt Payments
	DSCR=
	Annual Debt Payments
	Net Operating Cash Flow
	​


Typical thresholds:

Minimum: 1.20x

Comfortable: 1.30–1.50x

👉 This means the bank will size the loan so that annual payments are no more than ~65–80% of free operating cash flow.

This is why loans are rarely a straight % of revenue.

2. Cash flow definition (what banks actually look at)

Banks usually calculate something close to:

EBITDA

minus taxes

minus existing debt payments

minus owner drawings (for small PYMEs)

If financials are weak or informal, banks may:

Rebuild cash flow from bank statements

Use SAT filings (CFDI, declaraciones)

Apply conservative adjustments

3. Revenue does matter — but indirectly

Revenue is mainly used to:

Assess scale and stability

Cross-check margins

Detect volatility or seasonality

Typical informal heuristics (not rules):

Working capital lines: 10–30% of annual revenue

Short-term credit: 1–3 months of sales

Term loans: sized to cash flow, not revenue

So revenue is a sanity check, not the sizing driver.

4. Collateral & guarantees (very important in Mexico)

Mexican SME lending is still collateral-heavy.

Common collateral:

Real estate

Machinery / equipment

Vehicles

Accounts receivable (less common for small PYMEs)

Typical loan-to-value (LTV):

Real estate: 50–70%

Equipment: 40–60%

Vehicles: 30–50%

If collateral is strong, banks may:

Increase loan size

Relax DSCR slightly

Offer longer tenors

Personal guarantees from owners are almost always required.

5. Credit history & formality

Banks heavily weigh:

Credit bureau (Buró de Crédito)

SAT compliance and tax consistency

Years in operation (3+ years preferred)

Legal structure (S.A. de C.V. preferred over informal)

A strong credit profile can compensate for:

Lower margins

Shorter operating history

6. Sector & purpose of the loan

Loan sizing depends on what the money is for:

Purpose	How size is determined
Working capital	% of sales / inventory cycle
Equipment	% of asset value + cash flow
Expansion	Projected cash flow
Refinancing	Existing debt service

Riskier sectors (restaurants, construction, retail) face tighter limits.

7. Government-backed programs (NAFIN, Bancomext)

If a PYME qualifies for:

NAFIN guarantees

Supply-chain financing

Banks may:

Lend more

Accept lower collateral

Use revenue-based sizing more aggressively

TL;DR

Mexican banks do not lend based on revenue alone.

They primarily use:

Cash flow & DSCR

Existing debt obligations

Collateral & guarantees

Credit history & tax compliance

Revenue is used as:

A validation metric

A ceiling for working capital

A risk indicator

In Mexico, bank lenders evaluating SMEs (not large corporates) generally do not rely on a single formula like “X% of revenue.” Instead, they use a credit capacity framework anchored in cash-flow repayment ability, with collateral and formalization level acting as strong modifiers. That said, there are fairly consistent market norms you’ll see across banks (BBVA, Banorte, Santander, Citibanamex, regional banks) and Sofomes.

Below is how it typically works in practice.

1. Primary Driver: Cash Flow–Based Lending (Most Important)

Mexican SME lending is fundamentally repayment-capacity driven.

a) EBITDA / Cash Flow Coverage

Banks estimate free cash flow available for debt service and apply a minimum Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR).

Typical standards

DSCR requirement: 1.2x – 1.4x

More conservative banks: up to 1.5x

Government-backed programs (NAFIN, Bancomext): sometimes 1.1x – 1.2x

Formula (simplified):

Max Annual Debt Service ≤ Cash Flow / DSCR


Then they back into a loan amount based on:

Interest rate

Term (usually 12–60 months for SMEs)

b) Cash Flow Proxy (When Financials Are Weak)

For many SMEs with limited accounting quality, banks use proxies:

Bank statement inflows (last 6–12 months)

VAT filings (IVA trasladado vs acreditable)

SAT declarations (ISR base)

POS sales data (for retail)

This is very common in Mexico due to informality.

2. Revenue-Based Heuristics (Used as Secondary Checks)

Banks often apply rules of thumb as caps, not primary drivers.

Typical Revenue Multiples

Working capital loans:
10% – 30% of annual revenue

Revolving credit lines:
1–3 months of average monthly sales

Very formal SMEs with strong history:
up to 40% of annual revenue (rare)

⚠️ Revenue alone is never sufficient — margins and cash conversion matter too much.

3. Asset-Based Lending (When Collateral Exists)

Collateral can expand loan size but rarely replaces cash flow analysis.

a) Real Estate

Loan-to-Value (LTV):
50% – 70% of appraised value

Commercial property usually capped closer to 60%

b) Equipment / Machinery

LTV: 40% – 60%

Heavy discount due to resale risk

c) Receivables (Factoring / Crédito Simple con Garantía)

70% – 85% of eligible receivables

Strong preference for:

Large, well-rated buyers

Government entities

4. SME Leverage Limits (Implicit, Not Always Stated)

Banks typically enforce soft leverage ceilings:

Metric	Typical Range
Total Debt / EBITDA	2.5x – 4.0x
Senior Debt / EBITDA	≤ 3.0x
Short-term debt / Total debt	Closely monitored

Higher leverage is possible with:

Government guarantees (NAFIN)

Strong collateral

Long operating history

5. Formalization & Behavior (Extremely Important in Mexico)

Two SMEs with identical financials can get very different loan sizes.

Positive Adjusters

≥ 2–3 years operating history

Clean Buró de Crédito (company + shareholders)

Consistent SAT filings

Payroll via IMSS

Banking relationship history

Negative Adjusters

Cash-heavy business

Underreported revenue

Tax discrepancies

Prior restructuring

6. Typical SME Loan Size Outcomes (Real-World)

For a “normal” formal SME:

SME Profile	Likely Max Loan
MXN 5–10M revenue	MXN 500k – 2M
MXN 20–50M revenue	MXN 3M – 10M
Strong collateral	+30% to +70% uplift
NAFIN guarantee	+20% to +50% uplift
7. Summary: What Actually Determines the Loan Amount

In order of importance:

Sustainable cash flow & DSCR

Quality of financial information

Existing leverage

Collateral (optional but powerful)

Behavioral + tax compliance

Revenue (as a cap, not a driver)

There is no universal %, but if you want a mental model:

Mexican banks lend SMEs an amount whose annual debt service equals ~65–80% of sustainable free cash flow, subject to revenue and collateral caps.

Longer, practical answer 👇

Lenders do loan to cash-flow-negative companies when there’s a clear reason the cash flow is negative and a credible path to repayment. What matters is why you’re negative and what backs the loan.

When lenders are more willing
They’re much more open if one or more of these are true:

Temporary or explainable losses

High growth phase (hiring, marketing, expansion)

One-time investments (new facility, equipment, R&D)

Seasonal businesses with predictable swings

Strong revenue momentum

Revenues growing fast even if cash flow isn’t positive yet

Signed contracts, backlog, or recurring revenue

Asset backing

Real estate, equipment, inventory, receivables

In these cases, repayment relies more on collateral than cash flow

Equity support

Recent or committed equity raise

Founders or investors willing to inject capital if needed

Personal or corporate guarantees

Especially common for banks and SBA loans

Reduces lender risk when cash flow is weak

When lenders usually say no
Ongoing negative cash flow with no clear inflection point

Declining revenue

Thin margins and high burn

No collateral, no guarantees, no investor backing

Who is most likely to lend anyway
Different lenders, different risk tolerance:

Venture debt lenders → common for VC-backed, cash-burning startups

Asset-based lenders → focus on collateral, not profitability

SBA lenders → possible, but they’ll scrutinize projections hard

Private credit / alternative lenders → higher rates, looser rules

Banks → usually the toughest unless there’s strong collateral or guarantees

What they’ll really focus on
Even if cash flow is negative, lenders zero in on:

Projected cash flow (not just historical)

Burn rate & runway

Unit economics (are you losing money on purpose or structurally?)

Exit or repayment source
